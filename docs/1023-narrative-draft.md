## Activities Narrative

Beacon Institute for Global Catastrophic Risk ("Beacon") operates as a fiscal sponsor for charitable, educational, and scientific projects working to understand and reduce risks that could cause death or suffering at the scale of a billion or more people. These risks include unsafe or misaligned artificial intelligence, engineered pandemics and dual-use biotechnology, and emerging technology threats that intersect with these domains.

Beacon's core activity is receiving and administering charitable funds on behalf of sponsored projects that lack their own tax-exempt status. Researchers, educators, and policy professionals working on catastrophic risk reduction frequently have fundable, mission-critical work but face a choice between spending six to twelve months incorporating their own nonprofit or operating without appropriate legal and financial infrastructure. Beacon eliminates this bottleneck by providing 501(c)(3) status, financial administration, compliance support, and governance guidance, allowing project leads to focus on substantive charitable, educational, and scientific work. Beacon evaluates every prospective project against a concrete threshold: does this work plausibly reduce the probability or severity of a catastrophe affecting a billion or more people? Projects that do not meet this standard are redirected to better-suited sponsors.

All sponsored projects operate under written fiscal sponsorship agreements requiring that funds be used exclusively for charitable, educational, or scientific purposes consistent with Section 501(c)(3). Beacon conducts pre-grant due diligence on all projects, monitors the use of funds through quarterly reporting, screens all recipients against OFAC sanctions lists, and retains complete financial records for a minimum of seven years. The board of directors reviews a summary of all active sponsorships at regular meetings.

## Representative Project Types

Beacon expects to sponsor projects across the following categories. These are illustrative, not exhaustive; the common thread is a credible connection to reducing global catastrophic risk.

- **AI alignment education and talent development.** Intensive residential seminar and fellowship programs that recruit promising newcomers to AI safety and provide structured, mentored education in technical alignment research. One such program currently in Beacon's pipeline operates month-long cohorts of up to 30 participants, providing a curriculum developed in consultation with leading alignment researchers, structured around peer teaching, supervised study, lectures, and collaborative epistemics exercises. The program is free to participants, with accommodation and meals provided, removing financial barriers to entry. Participants who demonstrate strong aptitude and collaborative capacity may be selected for extended fellowships of up to twelve months, during which they receive grant funding to continue alignment research. Programs of this kind have a direct educational and public-benefit function: they expand the pipeline of researchers equipped to work on what is widely regarded as one of the most important and neglected technical challenges of the coming decades.

- **Technical AI safety research.** Independent or small-team research programs investigating alignment, interpretability, robustness, and evaluation of AI systems, particularly where the work is too early-stage, cross-disciplinary, or unconventional to fit within established academic or institutional settings.

- **AI governance and policy analysis.** Projects developing evidence-based policy recommendations, regulatory frameworks, and institutional design for the governance of advanced AI systems, including work engaging international bodies and cross-jurisdictional coordination efforts.

- **Biosecurity and pandemic preparedness.** Research and policy work on detection, attribution, and governance of dual-use biotechnology, including projects at the intersection of AI capabilities and biological risk.

- **Cross-cutting field-building and coordination infrastructure.** Efforts that support the broader catastrophic risk ecosystem: forecasting platforms, inter-organisational coordination, institutional design for existential risk governance, and knowledge-sharing initiatives that connect researchers across risk domains.
