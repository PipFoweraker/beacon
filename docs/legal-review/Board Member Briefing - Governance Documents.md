# Board Member Briefing: Governance Documents

**Beacon Institute for Global Catastrophic Risk**
**March 2026**

## Context

Beacon is being established as a US 501(c)(3) fiscal sponsor for projects in catastrophic and existential risk, including AI safety. As part of incorporation and the IRS Form 1023 application, the Board needs to adopt a set of foundational governance documents. These have been drafted by outside counsel (PKK Legal).

This briefing summarizes what each document does and highlights the key structural choices. The Board will be asked to adopt all documents at its organizational meeting.

## The Documents

### 1. Bylaws

The bylaws are Beacon's operating constitution. Key structural choices:

- **No members.** Beacon is board-governed. The Board elects its own successors and fills its own vacancies. This is standard for operating nonprofits and gives the Board full control over governance.

- **Board size: 3+ directors.** The exact number is set by Board resolution. An odd number avoids ties.

- **President must be a director.** The President is the chief executive officer. Secretary and Treasurer cannot concurrently serve as President, so at minimum two people are needed to fill all required officer roles.

- **Meetings.** Can be held remotely (telephone or electronic platform). Regular notice: 7 days. Urgent notice: 48 hours. Quorum: majority of directors in office.

- **Written consent.** The Board can act without a meeting, but ALL directors must consent (standard under Delaware law).

- **Audit committee.** Required from day one. Enhanced independence requirements (no staff on the committee, etc.) kick in only if Beacon exceeds $2M in gross annual revenue.

- **Indemnification.** Broad protection for directors and officers acting in good faith, with advancement of expenses. The Board intends to obtain D&O insurance to make this protection meaningful.

- **Director removal.** By majority vote of directors, with notice and opportunity to be heard.

- **Fiscal year.** To be set by Board resolution. We propose the calendar year (January 1 - December 31).

- **Dissolution.** Assets distributed to 501(c)(3) purposes. Required by the IRS.

### 2. Conflict of Interest Policy

Required by the IRS for Form 1023. Closely follows the IRS model policy (Form 1023 Schedule).

- Covers all directors, officers, employees, and identified contractors
- Defines "financial interest" broadly (ownership, investment, compensation arrangements — direct or through family)
- Requires disclosure and recusal when conflicts exist
- Uses a "reasonable appearance" standard — if it could look like a conflict, treat it as one
- Gift limit: $50/year/source
- Each covered person signs an annual disclosure form
- The Board periodically reviews compensation and related-party arrangements

**Practical implication:** All initial directors will need to complete the disclosure form. The founder (Pip Foweraker) will need to disclose any interests related to Certes and other entities.

### 3. Document Retention Policy

Establishes how long Beacon keeps various categories of records. Driven by IRS requirements, state law, and operational good practice.

- Permanent: incorporation documents, bylaws, minutes, tax returns, financial statements, insurance records
- 10 years (after active period): revenue/expense records, contracts, grants, audit reports
- 3 years (after active period): employment records, routine correspondence, credit card receipts
- Includes a litigation hold provision (stop destroying records if legal proceedings are anticipated)
- The President administers the policy

**Practical implication:** Minimal burden — mainly requires that Beacon set up proper file organization from the start and doesn't destroy things prematurely. Staff handles day-to-day compliance.

### 4. Whistleblower Policy

Required by IRS best practices and expected by funders. Establishes a channel for reporting ethical and legal concerns.

- Covers board members, officers, employees, and volunteers
- No retaliation for good-faith reports
- Reporting path: supervisor -> board member -> audit committee
- Audit committee investigates and reports to the full Board
- Confidential reporting permitted
- Malicious or knowingly false reports are a disciplinary offense

**Practical implication:** The audit committee chair needs to be designated so there is a clear point of contact. For a 3-person board where the President can't serve on the audit committee at the enhanced level, this will be one of the other two directors.

### 5. Anti-Bribery and Anti-Corruption Policy

Adapted from a policy previously used at Ashgro (a similar fiscal sponsor in the AI safety space) to meet funder governance expectations, particularly those of Open Philanthropy / Coefficient Giving's GCR Capacity Building program.

- Zero-tolerance for bribery and corruption
- Covers all Beacon personnel plus sponsored project personnel
- Prohibits facilitation payments
- References FCPA and applicable international standards
- Positive obligation to report suspected violations
- Annual Board review

**Practical implication:** This is largely a statement of standards rather than an operational burden. The main operational requirement is that Beacon perform proportionate due diligence on sponsored projects and third parties.

## What the Board Needs to Do

At the organizational meeting, the Board should:

1. **Adopt** the Bylaws
2. **Adopt** all six policies (COI, Document Retention, Whistleblower, Anti-Bribery, Code of Conduct, Anti-Harassment)
3. **Set the fiscal year** to January 1 - December 31 (by resolution)
4. **Elect directors** (confirm initial slate)
5. **Appoint officers** (President, Secretary, Treasurer)
6. **Designate the audit committee**
7. **Complete** initial Conflict of Interest disclosure forms
8. **Authorize** the President to open bank accounts and execute necessary documents

### 6. Code of Conduct

A short document establishing baseline behavioral expectations for everyone associated with Beacon.

- Covers integrity, respect, conflict avoidance, resource protection, confidentiality, legal compliance, reporting, and public representation
- Applies to sponsored project personnel in Beacon-related activities
- Cross-references all other policies rather than duplicating them
- Where a sponsored project has its own code of conduct, the more protective standard applies

**Practical implication:** This is primarily a framing document. The substantive obligations are in the other policies; the code of conduct ties them together and sets the tone.

### 7. Anti-Harassment and Sexual Misconduct Prevention Policy

The most detailed of the governance documents. Designed to meet or exceed the highest standards across the US, UK, and Australia.

- Covers all Beacon personnel plus sponsored project personnel and third-party interactions
- Broad definitions of harassment, sexual harassment, sex-based harassment, and hostile work environment
- Six reporting channels including anonymous reporting
- Defined investigation timeline (acknowledgment within 3 business days, target completion within 30 business days)
- Interim protective measures during investigation
- Written outcomes to both parties
- Appeals process
- Explicit retaliation prohibition
- Proactive prevention requirements: risk assessment, annual interactive training, culture monitoring
- External reporting rights preserved (EEOC, EHRC, AHRC)
- Annual Board review with compliance reporting

**Practical implication:** The main operational requirements are (a) establishing an anonymous reporting mechanism, (b) conducting a risk assessment within 6 months of adoption, and (c) delivering training. Staff will handle implementation; the Board provides oversight.

## Funder Compliance Context

Major funders in the GCR space (particularly Open Philanthropy / Coefficient Giving's GCRCB program) require grantee organizations to meet minimum standards for organizational policies. Based on available information, these standards require at minimum:

- Anti-harassment / sexual misconduct prevention policy with complaint and reporting procedures
- Whistleblower protection
- Code of conduct
- Conflict of interest policy
- Financial management and controls
- Anti-money laundering provisions (for international disbursements)

The seven documents above (bylaws + six policies) cover all but two of these requirements. The remaining gaps are:
- **AML procedures** (for international disbursements and OFAC screening) — to be addressed with specialized advice
- **Financial controls narrative** — to be prepared as part of operational setup

These should be in place before submitting grant applications to GCRCB-funded programs.
